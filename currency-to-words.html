Currency to words, done correctly per currency

Not every currency splits into 100 cents — Japanese Yen has no minor unit at all, and Kuwaiti and Bahraini Dinars split into 1,000 (not 100). This currency to words converter handles each of the 65+ supported currencies with its correct subunit name and decimal precision, not a one-size-fits-all "dollars and cents" template.

This distinction matters more than it might seem for anyone working across borders — international invoices, cross-border contracts, or documentation for a multinational business. Getting a currency's subunit wrong isn't just a cosmetic mistake; it can make a formal document look unprofessional or, worse, genuinely ambiguous about the amount involved. A British Pound splits into Pence, not Cents. A Saudi Riyal splits into Halalas. A Russian Ruble splits into Kopecks. Each of these has its own correct name, and this tool uses the right one automatically based on which currency you select, rather than defaulting to "cents" for everything.

The decimal precision differs too, and this is where most simple currency converters get it wrong. Most currencies use two decimal places, matching the familiar dollars-and-cents pattern. But Kuwaiti Dinars, Bahraini Dinars, Iraqi Dinars, Jordanian Dinars, Omani Rials, and Tunisian Dinars all use three decimal places — meaning their smallest unit is a thousandth of the main currency, not a hundredth. And currencies like the Japanese Yen, Korean Won, Vietnamese Dong, and Chilean Peso have no minor unit in everyday use at all, so amounts round to whole units with no fractional cents shown.

This tool spells out the subunit in full — "and Fifty Cents," "and Twenty Pence" — which is the right format for contracts, legal writing, and formal correspondence where the full written-out amount needs to be unambiguous. If you specifically need the banking-standard fraction format instead (the "50/100" style used on physical cheques), use the Cheque Amount in Words tool, which follows that different convention on purpose.

Frequently asked questions

How many currencies are supported?

65+ world currencies, each with the correct major and minor unit names — Pence for GBP, Fils for AED, Kopecks for RUB, Paise for INR, and so on.

What's the difference between this and the cheque amount tool?

This tool fully spells out the subunit ("and Fifty Cents") for contracts and legal writing. The cheque tool uses the banking-standard fraction format ("and 50/100") instead, which is what appears on an actual cheque.
