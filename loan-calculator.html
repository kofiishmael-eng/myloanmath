How this loan payment calculator works

This loan calculator uses the standard amortization formula lenders use to compute a fixed monthly payment: M = P × r(1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1), where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate, and n is the number of payments. It correctly handles 0% interest loans as a special case, since the standard formula breaks down mathematically at exactly zero interest.

Amortized loans — the kind this calculator handles, and the kind almost every personal loan, auto loan, and mortgage uses — work differently than most people intuitively expect. The monthly payment stays exactly the same for the entire term, but the *mix* of that payment shifts dramatically over time. In the early months, most of each payment goes toward interest, with only a small slice reducing the actual balance owed. As the loan progresses, that ratio flips: more of each payment chips away at principal, less goes to interest, because interest is calculated on the remaining balance, which keeps shrinking. This is exactly why paying off a loan early saves more money than people expect — every extra dollar in the early years is avoiding a lot of future interest, not just a little.

Loan term length creates a real trade-off worth understanding, not just a preference. A longer term lowers the monthly payment, which is often necessary to qualify for or afford a loan at all — but it also means paying substantially more in total interest over the life of the loan, since interest keeps accruing on the outstanding balance for longer. A shorter term does the opposite: higher monthly payments, but meaningfully less paid in total interest. Neither is universally "better" — it depends on what monthly payment is actually sustainable versus what minimizes total cost.

The "Show yearly breakdown" button reveals exactly this shifting mix year by year — how much of that year's payments went to principal versus interest, and what balance remains. It's worth checking, since it makes the front-loaded interest pattern concrete rather than abstract.

💡
Pro tip

Making extra payments toward your principal — even small, occasional ones — can meaningfully cut your total interest paid, since it shrinks the balance that future interest is calculated on.

Common loan types this calculator works for

The amortization math on this page applies to any fixed-rate installment loan — the loan type just changes the typical rate, term, and what a lender looks at.

Personal loans are usually unsecured (no collateral), used for anything from debt consolidation to major purchases, typically running 2-7 years with rates that vary widely based on credit profile. Auto loans are secured by the vehicle itself, generally 3-7 years, with the car serving as collateral until the loan is paid off. Mortgages are the largest and longest-term version of this same math — see our dedicated mortgage calculator for the fuller picture including property tax, insurance, and PMI. Business loans and loans against property (using real estate as collateral for a non-mortgage loan) follow the same amortization pattern, generally with rates and terms set by the lender's assessment of business or property risk.

What lenders typically evaluate

Approval and the interest rate you're offered usually come down to a handful of factors: credit score (higher scores generally unlock lower rates), debt-to-income ratio (your total monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income — lenders typically want this well under 40-45%), income stability (steady employment history matters more than income size alone), and, for secured loans, the value of the collateral relative to the loan amount.

Documents lenders commonly ask for

Requirements vary by lender and loan type, but most applications ask for some combination of: government-issued photo ID, proof of income (recent pay stubs, W-2s, or two years of tax returns if self-employed), 2-3 months of bank statements, and — for secured loans — documentation of the collateral (vehicle title, property deed, or purchase agreement). Having these ready before applying speeds up approval meaningfully.

Frequently asked questions

How is my monthly loan payment calculated?

Using the standard fixed-rate amortization formula, based on your loan amount, annual interest rate, and term. The same math banks and lenders use.

Why does the yearly breakdown show more interest in early years?

Amortized loans front-load interest — in the first year you owe interest on nearly the full balance, so more of each payment goes to interest. As the balance shrinks, more of each payment goes to principal.

Does this work for 0% interest loans?

Yes — it's handled as a special case, since the general loan formula is mathematically undefined at exactly 0% interest.
